The Isolationists operate the Mages Guild's spellcrafting service.

The Spell Maker (sometimes styled SpellMaker) is a powerful tool for magic-using characters. Use of the Spell Maker is the first benefit you gain when joining the Mages Guild, and is also available at the Temple of Kynareth for members of at least rank 6. With the Spell Maker, characters can create customized spells with a wide variety of effects. For purposes of some quests, custom spells still cannot substitute for the standard, "circinate" spells.

For more information, see the main lore article.

Spell Maker Basics[edit]

Using the Spell Maker, you can create custom spells with up to three effects. Depending on which spell effects are selected, the parameters of these custom spells can be adjusted to a large degree. Each effect has one or more of the following adjustable parameters, and they govern how powerful and expensive the effect will become:

  • Duration - if an effect has a definite duration, it will be the number of in-game minutes (which are five real-time seconds) it persists on the target, given by a constant integer plus a level-dependent one.
  • Chance - when an effect has a chance defined, the total here becomes the percent-chance of the effect taking hold. Hostile effects also have to bypass resistances and immunities independently of this chance.
  • Magnitude - effects with a definite magnitude will help or harm the target in a quantifiable way, given by a constant range of points plus a leveled range.

You will also want to select the intended target of the spell. All of the effects get directed at the target. The more ambitious the targeting scope, the higher the multiplier of the spell's cost, per the following table:

Caster only 1.0x
By touch 1.0x
Single target at range 1.5x
Area around caster 2.0x
Area at range 2.5x

When deciding how far you can feasibly power up the spell, the casting cost in spell points is the most important constraint you have to reckon with. Fortunately, there is no chance of spellcasting failure in Daggerfall; the only downside to an expensive spell is that it costs lots of money to create and lots of magicka to throw.

Spell Cost[edit]

The Spell Maker presents you with a non-negotiable price for scribing the spell in the Spell Cost field. Being nothing more than a sum of the raw numbers from above, the Spell Cost is a yardstick for comparing the value of the various spell effects. For these numbers, refer to the table in the Effect Details section below. Neither skill in the effects' applicable schools of magic, willpower, nor mercantile affect the Spell Maker's fee.

Casting Cost[edit]

The Casting Cost in spell points is a derivation of the Spell Cost, above, that takes into account your skills. For spells with effects coming from multiple schools, the costs are computed separately and simply added together. Cheap spells are given a floor cost of 5 spell points.

Skill reduces casting cost linearly. To give a general idea of its behavior, at 10 skill, an effect's Casting Cost in spell points is exactly 25 percent of its Spell Cost in gold pieces. There is a linear, downward slope of 0.25 percent points per skill point gained, such that 90 increments later, at 100 skill, Casting Cost is 2.5 percent of Spell Cost.

Casting Cost as a function of Skill and Spell Cost
 Casting Cost = (0.275 - 0.0025 × Skill) × (Spell Cost)

Icon[edit]

Different icons, in various colors, can also be chosen for each spell to appear on the HUD while the spell is active. These are unimportant for spells which are instantaneous (such as attack spells, teleports, etc.), but it can be useful to color-code icons for various types of spells with lasting durations, since these icons start to blink on the HUD when a spell is about to expire. For instance, it can help to know at a glance that the spell that is about to expire is a levitation spell which is keeping the character from falling from a great height, rather than a regeneration spell.

Element[edit]

Offensive spells can have an element designated. Changing the element around does not impact the cost. Besides altering the spell's aesthetics, the element changes how certain enemies will resist it. The table in the Notes: Element-based attacks section lists members of the Bestiary known to have particular strengths and vulnerabilities against the elements.

Effects[edit]

Parameters[edit]

The spell effects available in the Spell Maker can replicate every circinate spell, with the addition of a high degree of flexibility. With the unique exception of Teleport, which has no parameters, every effect allows at least one of the Chance, Duration, and Magnitude parameters to be manipulated.

  • Premium: A few effects charge a premium, a cost hurdle simply for being there. To give some examples, Identify and Transfer have a minor premium, Detect has a large one, and the Casting Cost of Teleport is literally just the premium.
  • Constant: Parameters have an adjustable constant that does not check character level. Usually the constant is a bad investment past the very earliest levels, if ever, but depending on the effect, the character might need to be as high as level 13 before the leveled variable beats the economy of the constant.
  • Per-Level: The level-dependent variable reads, "1 per 1 level(s)," by default. If adjusted to "1 per 2 level(s)," the cost of this entire component becomes zero due to downward rounding, and one is left with only the cost of the constant and premium (if there is one). This is a valuable option for low-leveled characters who cannot leverage the leveled variable yet.
  • Magnitudes: When changing the Magnitude parameter, note that a variable range of 1-1 has the same casting cost as a range of 1-2. A range of 2-2 has the same casting cost as a range of 2-3, and so on. Again due to downward rounding, the extra single point of magnitude is free. The higher the character level, the more significant these freebie points become.
  • Duration: For every effect with two parameters, one of them is Duration. The cost of extending the duration is computed separately from the other parameter, through simple addition, which is a glaring oddity. This is to say, for instance, that extending a 100% Chance Spell Reflection effect by a minute costs just the same as extending a 1% Chance by a minute.

Notes[edit]

Effects that disallow other effects[edit]

Spell effects can be grouped into three logical sets. The first and third groups prevent each other from existing in the same spell.

  1. Caster only: Effects that force the target to be Caster only - all operate special flags, menus, and player stuff, such as Identify and Levitate.
  2. Any: Effects that accept any targeting scope - all benign, and typically left on Caster only, such as Chameleon and Heal.
  3. Offensive: Effects that force the target to be something other than Caster only - all designed to do something to enemies and utilize the element, such as Damage and Pacify.

In what is probably an oversight, the offensive effect of Silence can coexist with any effect, be given any target, and switched to any element.

Efficiency[edit]

  • Casting cost does not correlate in any way with willpower, the governing attribute for training in the six schools.
  • The linear behavior causes skill to really shine when it gets close to 100 and pushes the casting costs to the floor.
  • In contrast, spells' strength is very sensitive to the character's level at all times. Spells with a Chance parameter in particular are prohibitively expensive for new characters. Whereas, any high-level character can throw long Durations, certain Chances, and great Magnitudes almost regardless of training.

On account of all this, it's advisable to re-evaluate your grimoire occasionally as you advance. Spells which may have needed a large and expensive per-level Chance component might no longer need to be so large and expensive. Splitting the handiest effects into multiple spells with differing parameters can also be helpful. For example, a short-duration levitation spell is less efficient than a lengthy one when cast at a high level, but a short flight is sufficient and even preferable in typical dungeon environments. Create such variants to cover contingencies and take another look through the Spellmaker at effects you previously thought were a waste of magicka. Things might have changed.

Chance Parameter[edit]

  • Chance of effect should not be neglected with attack spells. Even the most powerful Paralyze spells will be of little help if powerful targets routinely make their saving throws against them.
    • Some spell effects, notably Paralyze and Soul Trap, are all-or-nothing. Chance for these spells should have precedence over Duration in most cases.

Magicka as an element[edit]

Most spells in schools other than Destruction (including offensive, defensive, and utility spells) have Magicka as their base elemental sphere. This creates a lot of confusion among players, as sometimes the Magicka sphere is called simply "Magic". It is therefore important to distinguish between Spell Absorption, Spell Resistance, and Elemental Resistance: Magicka:

  • Spell Absorption is an effect which nullifies any direct damage spell (i.e. any spell transmitting a Destruction-school effect, including Continuous Damage, Damage, Disintegrate, Drain, and Transfer - e.g. Fireball - but not a spell transmitting a non-damaging hostile effect, such as Paralyze or Silence), and adds the spell point cost of the spell to the target's current spell point pool, as long as the target has sufficient free/"empty" spell points to absorb the spell's full cost, with the cost calculated as the cost of the spell if the target had cast the spell. Spell Absorption may nullify "Area at Range", "Target at Range", "Area around Caster", "On Touch", or "Caster Only"-type effects. This effect can be gained by creating and casting a custom protective spell with the eponymous effect (in which case the effect will also be subject to a chance of failure), or by choosing the Spell Absorption special advantage during character generation (in which case the effect will always apply according to the conditions of the selected advantage: either at all times, only when the character is in darkness, or only when the character is in natural light).
  • Spell Resistance is an effect which nullifies any offensive spell (i.e. any spell transmitting a Destruction-school effect, including Continuous Damage, Damage, Disintegrate, Drain, and Transfer - e.g. Fireball - as well as any spell transmitting a non-damaging hostile effect, such as Paralyze or Silence). Spell Resistance may nullify "Area at Range", "Target at Range", "Area around Caster", or "On Touch"-type effects. This effect can be gained by creating and casting a custom protective spell with the eponymous effect, or by increasing your Willpower.
  • Elemental Resistance: Magicka is an effect which resists a Magicka-based offensive spell by nullifying or halving its damage. This effect can be gained by creating and casting a custom protective spell with the eponymous effect, or by choosing either the Magic Immunity or Magic Resistance special advantage during character generation.

This is why the Magic Resistance or Magic Immunity special advantages won't save you against your first imp's Wizard's Fire attack; only your Willpower and/or corresponding Fire elemental resistance/immunity will (possibly) protect you from this Fire-based spell! However, these special advantages should be of use against enemy spells like Silence or Sleep, since those are Magicka-based. Therefore, don't try to exploit the character generation system by taking Magic Immunity and then choosing Fire/Frost/Shock weaknesses to lower the game's difficulty dagger; once again, Magic Immunity will not override these other elemental weaknesses. Nevertheless, due to a frequently-exploited bug, Magic immunity/resistance/low tolerance/critical weakness apply also to Disease, Paralysis, and Poison effects. Thus, due to this bug, choosing Magic Immunity will also render you immune to diseases as well as any paralysis and poison effects. (In Daggerfall Unity, Magic immunity/resistance/low tolerance/critical weakness only refers to the Magicka element of spells, similar to the Fire, Frost, and Shock elements, and no longer provides immunity/resistance/low tolerance/critical weakness to Disease, Paralysis, or Poison effects.)

Aesthetics[edit]

Normally, you are not allowed to change a spell's element if it does not contain an offensive effect. You can trick this restriction by adding a qualified offensive effect in second or third place, setting the desired element, and then deleting that effect. The elemental sphere will remain as long as you do not add anything else. This is generally just for the aesthetics, like changing Light to Fire-based, for the fire casting animation and sound. Unfortunately, this trick will not work for a spell with three effects, for which there's simply no room to 'add then delete' the sphere-changing effect.

Useful Custom Spells[edit]

  • Create Item spell — This creates items with leveled materials that are salable. Mass production is possible in theory, and potentially addictive. It also supports martial training, because all sorts of weapons and ammunition can be created (and discarded) no matter where you may be.
  • Shield spells — These spells absorb damage. Custom versions are very adaptable to the character's level, spell points, and skill. Shield should never be combined with anything that has a Duration, so that other buffs will not interfere with restoring its power.
  • Damage (Area around Caster) — A specially-purposed spell to solve the problem of creatures being "underfoot" when jumping, swimming or levitating down.
  • Identify & Open - These effects have fundamentally different uses when customized to minimum-cost, versus what they do at the strongest leveled Chance the player can afford. A low-cost Identify can be used to display the wagon in the left-hand inventory menu before entering a dungeon, giving you full wagon access inside. A low-cost Open succeeds against any exterior door in-town. Meanwhile, also scribe strong versions of the spells that will succeed in doing what they were designed to do.
  • Invisibility (True) — Only available through the Spell Maker, this is exceptionally useful in specific types of dungeons, such as Human Strongholds, that contain enemies who cannot see through invisibility.
  • Teleport — Creating a spell bearing the Teleport effect is less expensive than purchasing the circinate spell of Recall. Likely because the effect lacks any parameters, the spellmaking process accidentally skips evaluating the Spell Cost, and one can pick a teleport spell up for free at any time from the Spell Maker. If you attempt to edit the effect, it gets updated to its appropriate cost of 320, however. The actual Casting Cost behaves normally, regardless.

Here are some examples of complementary combinations.

  • Water Breathing and Water Walking — usually needed in conjunction with one another.
  • Jumping and Free Action — non-athletic characters should use Jumping long-term, and Free Action is similarly cheap and useful.
  • Multiple Fortified Attributes - Agility and Luck function together in armed combat. Strength also polls pretty well in such situations.
  • Invisibility or Shadow, and Levitate — for a ghostly form.
  • Spell Absorption and Spell Resistance — It seems absurd (and those with the talent do not need to maintain absorption), but these effects are complimentary, with absorption paying for ongoing maintenance of resistance, and resistance ensuring protection in every way absorption cannot. They're especially important in high-magicka environments like laboratories and crypts.

Example of Spell Making[edit]

Making a beginner's Fire Ball:

  1. Find a Mages Guild member (or other NPC) who offers [ Make Spells ] as an option and click it.
  2. Under [ Spell Name:_____ ] type in the name, "Fire Ball", and hit Return.
  3. On the right hand side (for focusing the spell's target), click the large square box for the third choice: "Single Target".
  4. Above that, in the ICON box, repeatedly click that box until a red (or other suitable) icon appears.
  5. Click the very large square box filled with stars to Add Effect.
  6. Select, from the pull down list of effects, the general nature of the spell. For this example it's "Damage".
  7. Double click and select from the next pull down list the type of damage. For this example it's "Health". Hit Return.
  8. The next screen is complicated, showing: Magnitude [1]-[1]+[1]-[1] per [1] level.
You should read this as: Base damage 1 to 1, plus per level damage of 1 to 1 multiplied by the character's level.
There is really not a lot for a low level player to do here but click the square Exit box.

 9. Choose a spell base, that is the type of health damage. Here, in the first choice, is Fire Damage, so click its square box.

10. Note the spell's gold pieces cost and casting cost in magicka. Is it affordable, and can you cast it?

11. If everything looks to be in place, click the square Buy box; otherwise, click the square Exit box.
